What is an Exoplanet?

An exoplanet is any planet beyond our solar system. Most orbit other stars, but free-floating exoplanets, called rogue planets, orbit the galactic center and are untethered to any star

How Are Exoplanets Discovered?

Exoplanets are discovered using various methods, including the transit method, radial velocity method, and direct imaging. Each method provides unique data that contribute to our understanding of these distant worlds.
The first exoplanets were discovered in the early 1990s, but the first exoplanet to burst upon the world stage was 51 Pegasi b, a “hot Jupiter” orbiting a Sun-like star 50 light-years away. The watershed year was 1995. Since then we’ve discovered thousands more.

Why Study Exoplanets?

Studying the diverse range of exoplanets and planetary systems that have been discovered to date – from the small to the large, from those that appear Earth-like to the profoundly bizarre – not only helps us learn about how these particular systems formed and evolved, but provides essential clues towards understanding whether and where life might exist elsewhere in the Universe. These out-of-these-world systems additionally give essential clues to our understanding of how our own Solar System formed and how Earth was created.

What ways
do scientists use
to discover exoplanets?

Transit Method

Description
 Measures dips in a star's brightness as a planet passes in front of it, blocking some light.

Key Missions

  • Kepler Space Telescope: Focused on a specific patch of sky, discovering thousands of exoplanets.
  • TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite): Surveys the entire sky for transiting exoplanets, focusing on nearby stars.

Radial Velocity Method (Doppler Shift)

Description
 Detects wobbles in a star’s motion due to the gravitational pull of an orbiting planet, indicated by shifts in the star's light spectrum.

Key Missions

  • HARPS (High Accuracy Radial velocity Planet Searcher): Ground-based spectrograph that has confirmed many exoplanets.

Direct Imaging

Description
 Captures images of exoplanets by blocking out starlight to see the planets directly.

Key Missions

  • SPHERE (Spectro-Polarimetric High-contrast Exoplanet REsearch): An instrument on the Very Large Telescope for direct imaging.
  • James Webb Space Telescope (JWST): Studies exoplanet atmospheres and directly images some planets.

Gravitational Microlensing

Description
 Utilizes the gravitational lensing effect, where a foreground object magnifies the light of a distant star, revealing possible planets.

Key Missions

  • OGLE (Optical Gravitational Lensing Experiment): Discovers exoplanets through microlensing events.

Astrometry

Description
Measures the precise movements of a star to detect gravitational influences from orbiting planets.

Key Missions

  • GAIA: Maps the positions and movements of stars, aiding in exoplanet detection through astrometry.
